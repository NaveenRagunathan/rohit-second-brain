Most financial advisors charge 1% of AUM.
Works fine until the client has $10M.
A senior executive with $10M in assets, 401k, equity comp, pension lump sum, non-qual plans, sits across from you. 
They like you. They trust you. 
They just did the math on your fee.
At 0.7%, that's $70,000 a year. 
And they made a decision you'll never hear about.
They'll give you $4M to manage. 
Keep the rest somewhere else. Maybe a Vanguard account they run themselves. Maybe cash sitting in a brokerage they opened in 2019.
You don't know. You'll never know. 
Because they never told you.
A client paying 0.7% on $12M knows they're paying NetJets money annually. 
So they protect themselves. 
They keep assets away from you. 
Not because they don't trust you. 
Because the math gave them a reason to.
So now you're recommending a Roth conversion without seeing the full picture. 
Reviewing an estate plan with three accounts missing. 
Running retirement projections on half the assets.
You think you're doing great work. 
You are. On the half they showed you.
This is what AUM pricing actually does at high net worth levels. It doesn't just create a pricing problem. It creates an information problem.
Here's what fixed fee looks like instead.
Same client, $10M in assets. 
You charge $30,000 flat per year, regardless of what they keep with you versus elsewhere. 
That number doesn't move if they buy a $2M vacation home. 
It doesn't move if they take their pension as a lump sum. 
It doesn't move if they decide to self-manage part of their equity comp.
So they show you everything. The Vanguard account. 
The old 401k they forgot to roll over. 
The non-qual plan their last advisor never asked about.
Now you're advising on the full balance sheet. 
The Roth conversion math is right. 
The estate plan has all the accounts.
The retirement projection actually reflects their life.
The advice changes when the conflict disappears.
Most financial advisors are giving great advice.
Just not to the whole client.
