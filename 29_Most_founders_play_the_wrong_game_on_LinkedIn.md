Most founders play the wrong game on LinkedIn.
They come here to sell their product or service…
but end up posting LinkedIn hacks just to chase reach.
Plenty of likes.
Zero buyers.
Broke.
Here’s the better play ↓
👉 Imagine you’re running a meal prep startup.
Instead of posting “10 tips to grow on LinkedIn” 
(which your buyers don’t care about)…
you go deep into the buyer’s world.
Who’s buying?
"Busy professionals who don’t have time to cook."
So your content looks like this:
- “How to eat healthy when you’re working 12-hour days.”
- “The hidden cost of ordering Deliveroo every night.”
- “What nobody tells you about burnout and nutrition.”
Now when they book with you,
it feels like the smart choice.
Because you’ve been talking about their exact problems.
But here’s the catch:
These posts don’t go viral.
They don’t get 1,000 likes.
Sometimes they only attract “binge-watchers” 
→ the ones who read everything but never buy.
That’s why you also hit decision-makers.
Decisions makers are different:
- Sometimes it’s the professional buying for themselves.
- Sometimes it’s a parent buying for the family.
- Sometimes it’s HR buying meal plans for the whole office.
So you shape content for them too:
→ Cost & convenience (for parents).
→ ROI on productivity (for HR).
→ Health & energy (for the professional)
Then you layer in content that always works 
↳ shareable, timeless, trust-building:
“How food delivery companies fool you with hidden fees.”
“5 things to check before trusting a meal prep company.”
And finally, throw in attention hooks:
“Why 90% of ‘healthy’ food isn’t healthy at all.”
“The dark truth about nutrition labels.”
That’s the game:
↳ Go deep into your buyer’s world.
↳ Speak to the decision-makers.
↳ Mix timeless trust-builders with attention plays.
You won’t always get 500 likes.
But the 10 right people who read it?
They buy.
And in the early days, let’s be honest
 buyers matter more than likes.
🔥 Question: Would you trade 500 likes for 5 real buyers?
